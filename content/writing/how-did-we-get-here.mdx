In Minecraft there's an advancement called *How Did We Get Here?* — you earn it by having every status effect on you at the same time. It's absurd, nearly impossible to plan, and only ever happens as the accumulation of a lot of smaller accidents. Which makes it the most honest title I could pick for this story.

## It starts with Legos

When I was nine, my parents gave me my first Lego Star Wars set for Christmas — not knowing they were pushing me down a rabbit hole. I built everything, then built past the instructions. I even made Lego YouTube videos (still up somewhere, to my eternal embarrassment) — and I was the one editing them, cutting clips together at nine years old without thinking of it as a skill.

By eleven, I hit the first real wall of my life: Legos are expensive, and my imagination had outgrown my allowance. So I did what felt logical — I went looking for software that would let me build Legos on the computer, where pieces are free.

I never found it. I found something better.

## A world within the world

Minecraft. A game that is really a world inside the world, where the only limit is how much you're willing to build. I started on Minecraft Classic — limited map, and no way to save. I used to leave the computer running so I wouldn't lose my progress, sneaking back to check the machine was still on. Whatever that is, it's not casual play. Something in me had decided this world mattered.

And then I wanted more than the game would give me. So I opened its files.

Inside, I found `.jar` and `.class` files — crazy magic characters that somehow shaped the game. I didn't understand any of it, but I found images in there, edited them, and saw my edits appear *inside the game*. I had reinvented texture packs without knowing they existed. That's still one of my favorite feelings: discovering a door before anyone tells you it's there.

## One line in Blocks.java

Texture packs weren't enough room for creativity. I joined Ironcraft, a French forum, and learned to decompile the game — still with no idea that what I was doing had a name. Then came the *déclic*. Inside a file called `Blocks.java`, I copied an existing line, changed it, added it back.

A new block existed in the game.

I can't overstate what that moment did to me. The magic characters weren't magic — they were a material, like Lego bricks, except free and infinite. I could see my change standing in the world. I was twelve, and I had just written my first line of code without ever deciding to "learn programming."

From there it compounded: my own modded server, then working on some of the big French modded servers — communities of hundreds of thousands of players, built out of that same `Blocks.java` energy.

## The same move, every time

Looking back, the pattern was set before I ever knew it was a pattern: creativity hits a constraint, and instead of stopping, I go after the tool itself. Legos too expensive → look for software. The software doesn't exist → find a world made of free blocks. The world is too limited → edit its files. The files are compiled → decompile them.

Everything since — the freelance years, becoming CTO of ClinicPilot, building Cial — is that same move at larger scale. Cial is literally a workspace that can open its own files and change itself, which is to say: I eventually built the thing I was looking for at eleven, except the blocks are code and the world builds back.

So, how did we get here? A lot of smaller accidents, stacked. And somewhere, a computer left on overnight, holding a world I wasn't done building.
